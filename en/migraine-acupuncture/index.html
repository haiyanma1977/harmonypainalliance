Acupuncture for Migraine & Chronic Headache in Winter Garden, FL

How acupuncture has been studied for migraine and chronic headache — and how Harmony Pain Alliance connects you with quality clinics close to home.

Living with migraine and chronic headache

Migraine is far more than "a bad headache." For the people who live with it, an attack can bring throbbing pain, nausea, and a sensitivity to light and sound that makes an ordinary day feel impossible — and chronic headache can shadow work, family, and rest for weeks at a time. If that is your experience, or a loved one's, you are not alone: migraine is one of the most common neurological conditions in the world.

Many people manage migraine well with the care of their physician or neurologist. Others keep looking for something more to add to what they already do. This page explains one option some people explore alongside their existing treatment — how acupuncture has been studied for migraine and chronic headache, and how to find a qualified clinic if you choose to.

What the research says

Acupuncture is one of the more widely studied non-drug approaches for migraine and chronic headache, and researchers have examined it both for reducing how often migraines occur and for managing acute attacks.

A 2024 systematic review and meta-regression in Complementary Therapies in Medicine examined how the amount of acupuncture related to outcomes and reported a dose-response pattern between the number, frequency, and duration of sessions and migraine-attack frequency (Yang C et al., 2024, PMID 39243985). For acute attacks, a 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis in BMJ Evidence-Based Medicine examined acupuncture during migraine episodes and reported it was associated with greater benefit than sham acupuncture at two hours after treatment, though the authors rated the certainty of this evidence as low to very low (Wang Y et al., 2023, PMID 37419658).

Chronic tension-type headache has been studied as well: a 2025 meta-analysis in the Journal of Oral & Facial Pain and Headache examined acupuncture for tension-type headache (six randomized trials, 927 patients) and reported reduced headache frequency and higher odds of headache relief compared with sham, while noting variation across the included trials (Lin PT et al., 2025, PMID 40129422). And a landmark 2018 individual-patient-data meta-analysis in The Journal of Pain pooled data across chronic-pain conditions, including headache, and found acupuncture was associated with modestly better outcomes than both sham and no-acupuncture comparisons (Vickers AJ et al., 2018, PMID 29198932).

Across these studies the size of the effect varies, and several authors call for higher-quality trials. This research suggests acupuncture is an actively studied option for migraine and chronic headache — these citations describe what researchers have examined, not a promise of any particular result. It is not a substitute for medical care.

Working alongside your existing headache care

This matters, so we want to be clear: acupuncture is meant to complement, not replace, your existing headache care. Preventive medications, acute "rescue" medications, and the guidance of your physician or neurologist remain the foundation of migraine management. Much of the research above examined acupuncture used together with — or compared against — standard care, not as a reason to stop it.

In practice, a good acupuncture provider will ask which medications and treatments you already use, coordinate around your neurology care, and encourage you to keep your existing appointments. If a clinic ever suggests stopping a prescribed medication, that is a sign to step back and talk with your physician first.

What a first visit looks like

Across the Harmony Pain Alliance network, a first visit generally follows three steps. First, an intake assessment: the acupuncturist reviews your headache history, triggers, current medications, and what you have already tried, and talks with you about your goals. Second, a personalized plan: based on that assessment, the practitioner outlines an approach tailored to your pattern rather than a one-size-fits-all routine. Third, a conversation about treatment frequency: how many visits, and how far apart, varies from person to person and is reviewed as you go.
